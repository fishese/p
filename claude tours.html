Scotland
Edinburgh
Tue, 18 May
7:00 AM – 7:00 PM  (12 hrs)
⟐ Hybrid — Great DIY city · One tour for the unmissable

Edinburgh's Old Town is one of Europe's most walkable historic capitals. With 12 hours in port you can explore deeply on your own — the Royal Mile, St Giles' Cathedral, and the National Museum of Scotland are all free or very cheap. The tram from Newhaven pier reaches Princes Street in 15 minutes.

  • 1Get to the city: Lothian Trams run from Newhaven every 8 min to Princes Street (~15 min, ~£2.50). Bus No. 11 or 16 also serve the route. No need for a taxi.
  • 2Royal Mile walk: Start at Castlehill at the top and descend 1 mile to the Palace of Holyroodhouse. Every close and wynd hides something: Gladstone's Land (17th-century merchant house, free to peek), Lady Stair's Close, Canongate Kirk.
  • 3St Giles' Cathedral (free): Enter the heart of Scottish history — the Thistle Chapel inside is among the most ornate Gothic spaces in Scotland, far more impressive than it looks from outside.
  • 4National Museum of Scotland (FREE): On Chambers Street, just off the Royal Mile. Covers Pictish stones, Viking jewellery, Scottish inventions, and Dolly the Sheep. Easily 2 hours.
  • 5Greyfriars Kirkyard (free): The famous churchyard with Greyfriars Bobby's grave, and the inspiration for Hogwarts character names. Atmospheric in any weather.
  • 6Grassmarket area: Below the castle — independent shops, artisan food stalls, and Scottish wool goods. Great for authentic Scottish tablet (fudge) and Dundee marmalade.

It's the one genuine highlight near Edinburgh that you can't replicate solo. The tour pairs it perfectly with Edinburgh Castle.

Scotland
Aberdeen
Wed, 19 May
7:00 AM – 5:00 PM  (10 hrs)
🏰 Tour Recommended — Best sight needs transport

Aberdeen city is walkable from the port — the glittering granite buildings of Union Street, the David Welch Winter Gardens (Europe's largest indoor garden, free), and the quirky fishing village of Footdee are all accessible on foot. However, the undisputed highlight — Dunnottar Castle on its dramatic sea cliff — is 15 miles south and unreachable by bus in any practical timeframe. The city itself is worth a morning stroll, but plan your tour first.

  • 1Aberdeen Harbour & Fish Market: Walk the working docks in the morning — Aberdeen is one of Scotland's busiest fishing ports. Raw, atmospheric, and free.
  • 2Marischal College (free to view): The world's second-largest granite building, its Gothic façade gleaming with reflective quartz particles in sunlight. Extraordinary.
  • 3David Welch Winter Gardens (FREE): Massive interconnected greenhouses with tropical plants, cacti, and the famous talking cactus "Spike." Genuinely one of Aberdeen's best free attractions.
  • 4Footdee (Fittie): Walk to the harbour mouth to find this 19th-century fishermen's village — cramped, colourful cottages whose residents turn their front doors away from the sea. Completely unchanged.

Orkney Islands, Scotland
Kirkwall
Thu, 20 May
7:00 AM – 5:00 PM  (10 hrs)
⟐ Hybrid — DIY the town · Tour the UNESCO Neolithic sites

Kirkwall town is an exceptional DIY option — the cruise pier is practically in the town centre, just 5–10 minutes' walk from St Magnus Cathedral. The Romanesque cathedral (free), Bishop's Palace ruins, and Earl's Palace are all within 10 minutes. Save your morning for the town; afternoon for the Neolithic tour.

  • 1St Magnus Cathedral (free): Founded 1137, this red-and-yellow sandstone Romanesque cathedral is among the finest in Britain. Climb the tower for panoramic Kirkwall views.
  • 2Bishop's & Earl's Palace ruins (£6 each): Right next to the cathedral. The Earl's Palace (1607) is considered one of the finest pieces of Renaissance architecture in Scotland — hauntingly intact in its ruined state.
  • 3Tankerness House Museum (free): Excellent overview of Orkney's 6,000 years of history — Vikings, Neolithic people, WWII — in a beautifully preserved 16th-century merchant house.
  • 4Kirkwall's flagstone lanes: Simply wander — independent shops sell Orkney Cheddar, hot-smoked salmon, and Highland Park chocolate truffles (the distillery's confectionery range).

Important: Ship departs at 2:00 PM — you need to be aboard by ~1:00–1:15 PM at the latest. The spectacular Harris beaches and Callanish Stones are both 45–60 minutes by car from Stornoway: DIY is not practical on this short day unless you stay in town. Stornoway town itself, though, is wonderful and perfectly walkable from the pier.

  • 1Lews Castle Grounds (free): A 5-minute walk from the pier across the harbour. The Victorian Gothic castle (now a hotel) is private, but the forested grounds and loch-side paths are beautiful in spring morning light.

  • 3Harris Tweed Hebrides (if open): Stornoway has shops selling genuine Harris Tweed — the only cloth in the world still handwoven by islanders in their own homes under a protected designation.

County Donegal, Ireland
Killybegs
Sat, 22 May
8:00 AM – 4:00 PM  (8 hrs)
⟐ Hybrid — Tour the Cliffs · DIY the harbour for food

Killybegs is Ireland's largest fishing port — and that means extraordinary fresh seafood right off the boats. The town itself is charming in an unassuming way. Perfect for a DIY lunch after a morning excursion to the cliffs. Walk the harbour, watch the trawlers unload, and eat very well for very little.

  • 1Killybegs Harbour: Walk the working fish quays. The sheer volume of deep-sea trawlers here is extraordinary — Ireland's premier fishing port in full working operation.
  • 2Seafood lunch: The Sail Inn or Melly's on Main Street serve fish chowder made with that morning's catch — some of the freshest seafood in Ireland.
  • 3Killybegs town walk: The town is also renowned for hand-tufted Donegal carpets that have furnished Buckingham Palace and the White House. Look for craft shops on the main street.
  • 4Kilcar village (taxi, ~€15): A 10-minute drive west, this village is the centre of Donegal tweed weaving — independent studios sell cloth and knitwear directly.

⟐ Hybrid — Tour Giant's Causeway AM · DIY St George's Market PM

Belfast gets a full 12 hours and is excellent for independent exploration. With a morning tour returning by ~2 PM, the afternoon is perfectly timed for St George's Market (open Sundays 10am–3pm) — Belfast's legendary Victorian covered market with local food producers, bakers, and artisan stalls. One of the best food markets in all of Ireland.

  • 1St George's Market (Sunday!): Belfast's finest market — try Belfast baps, soda farls, artisan cheeses, smoked fish, and hot food stalls run by locals. A magnificent 1890s iron and glass building. Open 10am–3pm Sunday.
  • 2Cathedral Quarter: Walk the cobbled lanes around St Anne's Cathedral. Fine Victorian architecture, murals, and independent shops. St Anne's itself is free and houses the remarkable Titanic memorial.
  • 3Titanic Quarter (free areas): Walk the slipways, see the famous Harland & Wolff cranes, and the SS Nomadic (White Star Line tender). Free without entering the main exhibition.
  • 4Black taxi tour (self-arranged): ~£15 per person for a Peace Walls and murals tour in an independently-operated black taxi. More honest and flexible than a coach tour of the same area.

Ireland
Dublin
Mon, 24 May
7:00 AM – 7:00 PM  (12 hrs)
✓ Excellent DIY city — DART train makes it effortless

Dublin is the most DIY-friendly port on the entire cruise. The DART commuter rail runs directly from Dún Laoghaire pier to the city centre in 15 minutes (€3–4 each way, every 15 min). The city is compact, flat, and entirely walkable. With 12 hours here, you can see almost everything independently — and save your money for excellent food.

  • 1DART to city centre: Dún Laoghaire DART station is a 5-min walk from the pier. 15 min to Pearse or Tara Street stations — right in central Dublin. Simple, cheap, runs all day.
  • 2Trinity College & Book of Kells: Free to walk the campus; Book of Kells exhibit (~€16) includes the magnificent Long Room library — 200,000 ancient leather-bound books in a cathedral-like barrel-vaulted hall.
  • 3National Gallery of Ireland (FREE): Caravaggio, Vermeer, and a superb collection of Irish art including Jack Yeats. One of Europe's finest free galleries. One hour minimum.
  • 4Merrion Square: Walk around the elegantly restored Georgian square. Oscar Wilde's colourfully irreverent statue is in the park, opposite his childhood home at No. 1.
  • 5St Patrick's Cathedral (€9): The largest church in Ireland, 800 years old. Jonathan Swift served as Dean here. The medieval nave is remarkable.
  • 6Powerscourt Townhouse Centre: A Georgian courtyard on South William Street, filled with artisan cafés, cheese shops, and design studios. Excellent for a coffee break.

⟐ Hybrid — DIY Cobh town · Tour to Kinsale for food

The ship piers right in Cobh (pronounced "Cove") — a beautifully preserved Victorian harbour town where Titanic's last passengers boarded in 1912. St Colman's Cathedral dominates the hillside with one of the most photogenic Gothic Revival façades in Ireland. Cobh is entirely walkable from the pier.

  • 1St Colman's Cathedral (free): Walk up from the pier (10 min, steep) to this extraordinary neo-Gothic Victorian cathedral. The 47-bell carillon is the largest in Ireland — there may be a bell-ringing session. Views over the harbour from the terrace are superb.
  • 2Cobh Heritage Centre (€9): Tells the story of 2.5 million Irish emigrants who left from this harbour between 1848–1950, the Titanic's last port of call, and the Lusitania tragedy. Genuinely moving.
  • 3Self-guided Titanic Trail (free): A walking route map (available at the Heritage Centre) links all Titanic-related buildings in Cobh — the White Star offices still stand exactly as they were in 1912.
  • 4Cobh waterfront walk: The colourful Victorian terraced houses rising above the harbour are uniquely photogenic. Walk east along the waterfront to the Cathedral and back — the whole town is really one great picture.

Dartmouth is one of England's most beautiful small towns — a medieval port frozen in architectural amber, with the River Dart curling through it. Narrow cobbled lanes, painted Tudor buildings, and a 14th-century castle at the harbour mouth. The ship arrives at 11 AM and departs at 8 PM — 9 glorious hours. Take a tour in the afternoon and spend the rest simply being in the town.

  • 1Dartmouth town walk: Foss Street and Duke Street into the medieval town. Painted timber-framed buildings date to the 1600s. St Saviour's Church (1372) has a beautiful carved stone rood screen.
  • 2Bayards Cove Fort (free): A tiny 16th-century gun fort at the water's edge, completely preserved. Film-set beautiful — it appeared in the TV series The Onedin Line. One of England's most atmospheric small fortifications.
  • 3Dartmouth Castle (£6): Walk 20 minutes along the riverbank path to this 14th-century castle at the harbour mouth. Extraordinary river and sea views — one of the finest coastal positions in Devon.
  • 4Kingswear by ferry: Take the passenger ferry (~£2) across the river mouth for the best view of Dartmouth's Georgian waterfront — and a completely different perspective on the town's setting.
  • 5Royal Avenue Gardens (free): Formal gardens overlooking the river — the kind of perfectly maintained English coastal garden where sitting on a bench for 20 minutes is entirely justified.

Devon cream teas are serious business: cream goes first, then jam (the opposite of Cornwall). The Dartmouth Tea Company or Alf Resco on the waterfront are the places for this essential experience. Fresh crab from the Dart estuary is exceptional.

The River Dart cruise combined with a Victorian steam railway is quintessentially British and exists nowhere else in this form — gliding through one of Devon's most beautiful valleys, then rattling along a coastal steam line past ancient woodland. Agatha Christie's Greenway estate is visible from the water.
