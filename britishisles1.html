Day 3  ·  Tue May 18  ·  7 AM – 7 PM

Edinburgh

Newhaven, Scotland

DIY

12 hrs in port

What to see

Arthur's Seat — free volcanic hike with panoramic city views (~30 min up; stunning in May when the gorse is in bloom)

National Museum of Scotland — Celtic gold treasures, natural history, free entry; easily two hours

Victoria Street — the winding medieval lane that inspired Diagon Alley; also Grassmarket below it

Royal Mile — cobblestone history, free to walk; Edinburgh Castle interior costs ~£28pp if you want inside

Local food finds

Cranachan — raspberries, toasted oats, cream: Scotland's dessert, found at any good pub
Haggis, neeps & tatties — Scotland's national dish; try it at a Royal Mile pub
Scottish tablet — grainy sweet fudge, firmer than fudge, sold at every market stall
→

Lothian Bus from Newhaven Harbour to Princes St: £2.50, 25 min. Edinburgh has 12 hours and excellent public transit — it's one of the world's most walkable cities and requires no cruise excursion.

Day 4  ·  Wed May 19  ·  7 AM – 5 PM

Aberdeen

Scotland

DIY

10 hrs in port

What to see

Dunnottar Castle — a 14th-century ruin on sheer sea cliffs, one of Scotland's most dramatic sights. Hire a local taxi from the pier (~£30 return, 15 miles south)

Old Aberdeen & King's College — medieval cobblestones and a 15th-century university quad, free to walk

Aberdeen Harbour — genuine working port with colourful trawlers and the silver granite cityscape behind

Local food finds

Aberdeen Rowie (Buttery) — a flaky, salty bread roll found ONLY in Aberdeen; get one at any bakery before 10 AM
Aberdeen Angus beef — the original, from the actual source
→

Port is walkable to the city centre. Dunnottar Castle is spectacular and entirely DIY — negotiate a return trip with a local taxi at the pier. Oceania's ABE-002 "Castle and the Coast" ($119pp) covers the same ground if you prefer a guided option.

Day 5  ·  Thu May 20  ·  7 AM – 5 PM

Kirkwall

Orkney Islands, Scotland

Use credit

$149 pp

10 hrs in port

KOI-001  ·  4 hrs  ·  departs 9:00 AM or 12:00 PM

Ancient Treasures of Orkney

$149

This is your top-priority port for shore credits. The UNESCO Neolithic sites — Skara Brae, Ring of Brodgar, Stenness — are 20–25 km from Kirkwall with no convenient public transit. A guide is essential to bring 5,000-year-old sites to life, and the cruise excursion guarantees your return time to the ship.

What to see

Skara Brae — a 5,000-year-old Neolithic village with stone beds and dressers still intact. Older than Stonehenge by 1,500 years; discovered under sand dunes in 1850

Ring of Brodgar — a vast ceremonial stone circle in wild moorland, set between two lochs; raised around 2500 BCE

Stones of Stenness — Britain's oldest standing stones (~3100 BCE); four tall angled slabs that predate every other monument on the island

St Magnus Cathedral (free, founded 1137) — a 5-min walk from the pier; extraordinary Romanesque interior, visit before or after the excursion

Local food finds

Orkney Cheddar — rich and distinctive; sold along Kirkwall's main street
Smoked Orkney salmon — from island smokehouses, among Scotland's finest
Orkney ice cream — made with local cream; find it near the harbour
→

Take the 9:00 AM departure to maximise your day. St Magnus Cathedral (free) is right at the pier — visit on your own before or after. The Earl's and Bishop's Palace ruins are also a short walk and cost only £5pp.

Day 6  ·  Fri May 21  ·  7 AM – 2 PM

Stornoway

Isle of Lewis, Outer Hebrides

Use credit

$139 pp

7 hrs in port

SYY-002  ·  3 hrs  ·  departs 7:30 AM

Scenic Lewis

$139

Only 7 hours in port, and the prehistoric sites and blackhouses are 12–20 miles away. Local taxis are scarce when a full ship arrives. SYY-002 departs at 7:30 AM and finishes by ~10:30 AM, leaving time to explore Stornoway town before the 2 PM departure.

Upgrade option: SYY-001 "Breathtaking Harris" ($259) — takes you to the Isle of Harris for stunning mountain scenery, Luskentyre Beach (one of Europe's most beautiful), and a Harris Tweed factory visit. More scenic, $120 more.

What to see

Calanais Standing Stones — a cruciform megalith arrangement from 2900 BCE, older than Stonehenge (SYY-002 passes by; consider a local taxi post-excursion for a longer stop if time allows)

Arnol Blackhouse — a preserved traditional Hebridean long-house, inhabited into the 1960s, showing how islanders lived for centuries in these low-walled thatched structures

Lewis moorland — the largest stretch of blanket peat bog in the UK; an otherworldly, treeless landscape unlike anywhere else in Britain

Local food finds

Stornoway Black Pudding — Protected Geographical Indication: only black pudding made on Lewis can bear this name. Find it at a local butcher or the Co-op to take back aboard.
Lewis lamb — salt-marsh flavour from Hebridean sheep; notably different from any mainland lamb
→

After the excursion (~10:30 AM), walk to Lews Castle grounds for harbour views. Finding Stornoway Black Pudding at a local butcher is worth the effort — it is one of the most location-specific food souvenirs on the entire voyage.

Day 7  ·  Sat May 22  ·  8 AM – 4 PM

Killybegs

County Donegal, Ireland

Use credit

$159 pp

8 hrs in port

K1L-002  ·  3.5 hrs  ·  departs 8:45 AM or 10:00 AM

The Wild Sliabh Liag Cliffs

$159

The cliffs are 14 miles from port and local taxis are scarce when the ship arrives. The excursion includes a guide with the site's ancient pilgrimage history, plus café time at the clifftop (scones and beverage included at Tí Linn Café & Craft Shop).

What to see

Sliabh Liag (Slieve League) — among Europe's highest coastal cliffs at 600m, nearly 3× taller than the Cliffs of Moher, and far less crowded

On a clear May day, views stretch across the Atlantic to Sligo town and the Mayo Mountains to the south

Killybegs Harbour — Ireland's largest fishing port; walk the pier after the excursion for a genuinely working-town atmosphere

Local food finds

Donegal seafood chowder — fresh Atlantic catch; try it at a harbour café before or after the cliffs
Irish soda bread — brown soda bread, baked fresh daily; Donegal versions are particularly good
→

Take the 8:45 AM departure. After returning around noon, walk the harbour and watch the fishing boats — one of the most authentic working-port scenes in Ireland, and completely free.

Day 8  ·  Sun May 23  ·  7 AM – 7 PM

Belfast

Northern Ireland

DIY

12 hrs in port

What to see

Titanic Belfast — world's largest Titanic exhibition, built on the actual construction slipways. Pre-book at titanicbelfast.com (~£28pp). Do not miss this.

Black Taxi Mural Tour — local drivers take you through Falls Road and Shankill Road, explaining both sides of the Troubles from lived experience (~£15–20pp, book direct online). Not offered as a cruise excursion — the independent version is far more personal and honest.

SS Nomadic — the only surviving White Star Line vessel, moored beside Titanic Belfast

Cave Hill — free hike above the city; the basalt cliff profile is said to have inspired Swift's Giant Gulliver

Local food finds

Ulster Fry — Northern Irish full breakfast; soda farl and potato bread make it distinct from any other fry-up
Yellowman — crumbly honeycomb toffee, traditional to Ulster; find it at St George's Market if open
Belfast bap — soft floury roll; fill it with whatever is local at a market stall
→

Pre-book both Titanic Belfast and a Black Taxi Tour before you sail. The taxi tour gives a raw, personal account of the Troubles that no cruise bus can replicate. With 12 hours you have time for both, plus the Cathedral Quarter.

Day 9  ·  Mon May 24  ·  7 AM – 7 PM

Dublin

Dún Laoghaire, Ireland

DIY

12 hrs in port

What to see

Book of Kells, Trinity College — an 800 AD illuminated manuscript, one of the greatest medieval artworks surviving. Pre-book at tcd.ie (~€25pp)

Kilmainham Gaol — where the 1916 Rising leaders were imprisoned and executed; one of the most moving sites in Ireland. Pre-book at heritageireland.ie (~€9pp)

National Museum of Ireland – Archaeology (FREE) — Celtic gold, Viking Dublin artefacts, and the famous bog bodies; easily 1.5 hours

Dún Laoghaire East Pier — a beautiful harbour promenade right from the ship, free and local

Local food finds

Dublin Bay Prawns — sweet and fresh, caught just outside the bay
Brown soda bread with smoked salmon — the definitive Dublin café lunch
Coddle — Dublin's own stew of sausages, bacon, and potatoes
→

DART train from Dún Laoghaire Station (2 min walk from the pier) to Pearse Street / city centre: 25 min, ~€4 return. This is the easiest DIY port on the trip — 12 hours and a direct rail link straight into Dublin.

Day 10  ·  Tue May 25  ·  9 AM – 5 PM

Cork

Cobh, Ireland

DIY

8 hrs in port

What to see

English Market, Cork City — a Victorian covered food market established in 1788, FREE to enter. One of the finest food markets in Europe; this alone justifies the train ride

Cobh Heritage Centre — the Queenstown Story: Irish emigration, the Titanic's last port, and Annie Moore (the first emigrant processed at Ellis Island) (~€9pp)

St Colman's Cathedral, Cobh — magnificent neo-Gothic spire rising above the harbour from every approach; free to enter

Cork Butter Museum — Cork was the world's centre of butter export for 200 years; a genuinely surprising and entertaining small museum (~€8pp)

Local food finds

Drisheen — Cork blood sausage found almost nowhere outside Cork city; try it at the English Market (Kay O'Connell's stall is legendary)
Tripe & Drisheen — Cork's most local traditional dish; a genuine only-found-here experience
Cork farmhouse cheeses — Ardsallagh, Coolea, and Gubbeen at the English Market stalls
→

Train from Cobh to Cork City: 20 min, runs every 30 min, ~€5 return. Go to Cork first — 2–3 hours at the English Market and city centre — then return to Cobh in the afternoon to explore the colourful harbour town.

Day 11  ·  Wed May 26  ·  11 AM – 8 PM

Dartmouth

Devon, England

DIY

9 hrs in port

What to see

Dart River Ferry to Totnes — a 1.5-hour scenic cruise through wooded Devon valleys past Agatha Christie's Greenway Estate (~£15pp; tickets at the Boat Float ferry landing, 2 min walk from the ship)

Bayard's Cove — a perfectly preserved medieval quayside with original 16th-century houses, free to explore and one of England's most picturesque spots

Dartmouth town — Foss Street and the Butterwalk arcade are beautifully intact and free to wander

Dartmouth Castle — 15th-century sea fortress at the estuary mouth with cannon views over the river (~£8pp)

Local food finds

Devon Cream Tea — scones + Protected Designation of Origin clotted cream + jam + tea. In Devon, cream goes on BEFORE the jam (a matter of fierce regional identity vs Cornwall).
Fresh Devon crab — local crab sandwiches at a harbour café; Dartmouth is surrounded by working crab boats
→

The ship docks right in town — walk off and you're immediately in one of England's most beautiful harbours. Buy the independent ferry ticket to Totnes at the Boat Float. Credits are all spent by now: this is a day for wandering slowly and eating well in the long May evening light.
